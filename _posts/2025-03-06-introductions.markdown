## Where do we start?

What am I doing? At the moment, I don't completely know.

I've started working on AI. With LLMs. Grappling with NeuroAI. Trying to understand "intelligence" — and having a chuckle at how the more we claim that as our destination, the more we question where exactly we're going. What do we expect to find? Feels personally relatable.

My approach has been to learn by doing — developing and working on (hopefully) potent research ideas. I plan to use this space to track my progress in the open. But I haven't left my previous work behind. If anything, I'm trying to make it all make sense as a single journey. No forks in the road, just...lots of twists and turns.

## The biggest turn yet

I didn't come from a conventional AI background, but I've always been interested in cognition. Looking back, in my PhD application, I wrote about how we needed not only new tools but also new philosophies to interrogate the inner workings of the brain. Admittedly, I lost sight of that vision during my PhD training — or maybe I never really knew what it entailed in the first place.

Full disclosure: I was a skeptic. I didn't follow the machine learning field for a long time. It seemed useful, but I thought — hoped — cognition was more than math. More than algorithms, statistics, and linear algebra. A part of me still believes that, perhaps fallaciously.

## Is this the right way?

Despite AI/ML's modern successes, we still have no concrete clue how sentience arises. So, is this the right path? Or just an elaborate imitation? Or maybe, the missing ingredient is simply time.

Perhaps we'll look back one day and find all the progress right now was just pebbles. A lot of pebbles. But cognition is enormously complex. What if it surpasses our ability to comprehend? Even for the most brilliant minds, armed with the most powerful tools, what if the principles underlying cognition appear infinite?

Can we ever reach our destination? What if the mound of pebbles is larger than any eye can see, yet the mountain would cease to exist without a single one of them? Can anyone fathom the planet by counting each of its atoms?

## Are we there yet? Wait-, can we even get there?

There are surely even more fundamental rules to uncover — causes that shape what we observe. This is where I align my current path with my background in molecular self-assembly: to find more of these principles in the spatial, temporal, molecular, supramolecular, connectionist, or those gravitationally negligible yet poetically intriguing mechanisms from which cognition and consciousness emerge.

But to be excessively pedantic about what we often take for granted — in self-assembly, chemistry, the assembly of anything -- there are reactions and processes, the fundamental workings of atoms and nature that connect the components we begin with, the rules we define, and the protocols we follow with the outcomes we see. These intermediaries are frequently described in broad strokes, in proven scientific methods, in bulk, in terms of rates and equilibriums. Simplified. Because we don't need to account for every single thing that happens.

Except -- well, maybe this here. And then that. And sometimes those.

That's the nature of science: we create models, then refine them as we uncover exceptions and details. Our simplified framework grows more complex—but still not so complex. Yet, what happens when the complexity we must consider in the emergence of phenomena turns out to be, unavoidably and paradoxically, everything?

## Whatever, let's go!

And yet, I believe I'm here because AI is the closest anything has come to cognition. Not because of how it appears — if chatbots were people, they'd be savants -- but because it has forced me to adopt a colder, more pragmatic perspective. That cognition is (or could be), at its core, mathematical. That the only difference between an intelligent machine and a human brain is how squishy each is.

That perhaps I should discard some — but not all — of the romanticism of consciousness and the soul. Not all, because there is still something awe-inspiring about the idea that a mess of math could create something so vibrant -- life and our observation of it.

Intelligence once seemed like magic. Now, we are inching closer to revealing the trick. I want to do my part in cutting up the veil, even if I'm still figuring out exactly where to make the first incision.
